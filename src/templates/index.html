In this piece, we investigate crimes in the city of Berkeley. First, we delve into when and where crimes happen in Berkeley. Next, we specifically examine trends in traffic stops.

When and where do stops happen?

Who gets stopped?

Black and Hispanic residents continue to face higher rates of traffic stop and search incidents, according to data published by the Berkeley Police Department, or BPD.

In hopes of stopping this trend, Berkeley City Council made the decision to remove police officers from traffic enforcement and instead create a Berkeley Department of Transportation, or BerkDOT, in July. While the program has not been implemented yet, city council hopes to get it up and running within one year, according to East Bay for Everyone Co-Executive Director Darrell Owens.

“The goal of the program is to create a unique and hopefully revolutionary and transformative model that looks at traffic enforcement without using guns or police officers at the first line of interaction between public and enforcement,” Owens said.

According to Berkeley Copwatch volunteer Holly Allans, traffic stops are instances where racial profiling occurs” in Berkeley, pointing to a May 2018 report by the Center for Policing Equity. While Black residents make up about 8% of Berkeley’s population, they were stopped 36% of the time, leading to a 6.5 times likeliness to be stopped when compared to white drivers, according to the report.

The graph below shows the number of traffic stops per 100 people living in Berkeley, grouped by ethnicity. For instance, in 2016, the Berkeley Police Department stopped Black drivers 35 times out of every 100 Black people residing in Berkeley.

{% include "partials/line_stops.html" %}

When looking at recent public data, per 100 people living in Berkeley, Black drivers were stopped 14 times, Hispanic drivers were stopped 4 times and white drivers stopped 2 times in 2020. While this rate has dropped from a high in 2017, Black residents still make up the largest group of people stopped.

The graph below shows the number of car searches for every 100 stops. This demonstrates that bias may extend beyond mere stops to the searches themselves.

Recent public data from the BPD show that although the overall rate of traffic stops has dipped since 2019, Black people are still stopped at a higher rate compared to other ethnicities. Of individuals who were stopped, Black people were more than twice as likely to have their car searched as white individuals.

According to BPD spokesperson Officer Byron White, law enforcement has historically performed duties that are not always within their area of expertise. He added that officers can be overwhelmed by calls that they are not properly equipped to handle, ranging from animal control to assisting individuals who are mentally ill.

“I feel that law enforcement is the last place that people can call for help,” White said. “It’s been an issue nationwide for departments since it is just so easy to call 911. Many services stop at a certain point in the night and things will be referred to the police department, so officers are stuck there trying to figure out what to do about the issue.”

Amidst national calls to defund police departments, White agreed that there is room to make public safety more efficient. Bolstering parts of the city that are involved in safety like the Departments of Transportation, Health Services, Fire and even Finance, would help address some of the issues that end up with the police, according to White.

White said one example of these changes in action was the creation of the city’s Mobile Crisis Team under the Department of Health Services in the 1980’s. Having experts available to assist police officers in fields where they have informal training, White added, helps residents receive better care.

“Our training is helpful but it’s not a degree,” White said. “It won’t completely solve the person’s problem. Having the mobile crisis team helps individuals get the help they need that we as officers can’t always provide.”

A similar sentiment was echoed by local organizations who have advocated for the creation of the Department of Transportation and for other police reform.

East Bay for Everyone Co-Executive Director Darrell Owens discussed his support for a proposal put forth by Councilmember Ben Bartlett to remove police from mental health calls and have those wellness checks conducted by mental health officials.

In practice, Owens pointed to the existing Berkeley Fire Department and Berkeley Parking Enforcement Unit, both of which are made up of unarmed officials that specialize in what they do.

“It’s not about individually how the police department functions, it’s about the status quo of police in the United States that says police have to respond to all problems,” Owens said. “It’s not just about shifting duties from the police department to an unarmed department. It’s also about reexamining the applications of law.”

Ultimately, activism is also about reinvesting money saved on police in preventative solutions that can make streets safer for cyclists, motorists and pedestrians, according to Owens. These solutions, he said, include reducing cars to reduce traffic violations and making sure public transit is funded so that people don’t have to drive.

Allans also said in the email that it is also important to look into how traffic enforcement indirectly affects Black, Brown and poor community members, from those who are ticketed who live in their vehicles to those who lose their vehicles because they cannot pay the punitive fines.

She added that she is concerned about how other policies such as the revised use-of-force policy, because of the lack of specificity around how they will be implemented.

“Though there was a flurry of news reporting from nationwide and even international outlets about the proposal to remove police from traffic stops, there has been far less follow up,” said Allans in the email. “It is clear that in the face of great and necessary changes, institutions will not give up their power without a fight.”

A task force created by Arreguín in June focused on fair and impartial policing will tentatively come out with a list of proposed changes for the city council by October, according to Police Review Commission, or PRC, chair Kitty Calavita. Calavita, who also serves on the task force alongside PRC vice chair Nathan Mizell and PRC commissioner Ismail Ramsey, added that the main focus of the task force is identifying racial disparities in stops and searches.

Calavita also said that some features of policing that stood out were the facts that Black individuals were much more likely to be subject to traffic stops by officers and that they were also more likely to be searched after being stopped. Despite the higher rate of stops and searches, according to Calavita, the yield rates of searches for white individuals are higher.

The bar chart shows the consequences of the stops made by ethnicity.

{% include "partials/stacked_bar.html" %}

White drivers who are stopped receive citations are nearly double the rate of Black drivers. In fact, Black drivers are the most likely group to receive a warning, rather than a disciplinary consequence.

“These differences really make it seem like the threshold of suspicion for Black individuals is lower, and if that really is the case then we need to find a way to address that inequity,” Calavita said.

In addition to the task force, Calavita pointed out the creation of BerkDOT as a potential step forward. Transitioning away from armed law enforcement could make a difference in the outcomes of stops and improve relations between the police and communities of color, according to Calavita.

While the new department could help address some of the disparities that exist today, Calavita said that the success of the program would lie in how it is implemented. She added that the momentum of demonstrations across the nation have influenced recent city council actions like the creation of the task force or the passage of the resolution creating BerkDOT.

“We have a lot of momentum for change, and this is the time to act on that,” Calavita said. “Hopefully some very progressive outcomes and proposals will come out of it, but we will have to see how these reforms will materialize.”
